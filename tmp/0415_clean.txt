This yields a two-stage interpretation of the hierarchy. At the group level,κ g determines
the maximal fraction of raw signal that coordinates in groupgmay retain after shrinkage;

The grouped normal-means representation is introduced for analytical tractability and to
make the shrinkage geometry explicit. The resulting coordinate-wise retention interpretation
is exact in that representation, but should be viewed as a structural characterization of
the hierarchy rather than a literal coordinate-wise identity for a general non-orthogonal
regression design.
1.7 Prior on the allowance parameter
A convenient default, and the one adopted in the theoretical development below, is
κg ∼Beta(α κ, βκ), α κ, βκ >0.(10)
This choice is analytically tractable, flexible on (0,1), and particularly well suited to quan-
tifying prior mass near the null regimeκ g ≈0.
Under the one-to-one correspondence (2), the Beta prior onκ g induces a prior on the
group-specific slab widthc g. One may therefore think either in terms ofκ g, which emphasizes
group-level allowance, or in terms ofc g, which emphasizes group-specific slab regularization.
These are mathematically equivalent parameterizations, but theκ g parameterization is more
natural for the present allowance theory.
The roles ofα κ andβ κ are distinct. The parameterα κ controls how much prior mass
accumulates nearκ g = 0, and therefore how strongly the prior favors aggressive group-level
suppression. The parameterβ κ shapes the upper tail on the unit interval and affects how
willing the prior is to retain substantial group-level allowance.

reasonable default when no strong prior information is available about within-group het-

augment the model with a priorσ 2 ∼Π σ.
1.10 Posterior computation
Conditional on the latent scales, the model remains Gaussian inβ, so posterior computation
inherits the main computational conveniences of global-local shrinkage priors. The hierarchy
is only partially conjugate: exact named full conditional distributions are available for the
Gaussian coefficient block and a subset of auxiliary variables, whereas several remaining
one-dimensional updates are available only through exact conditional kernels. In particular,
the coefficient block may be updated jointly under standard Gaussian formulas or sampled
with modern gradient-based methods.
For computation it is often convenient to work with transformed variables such as

recommend implementing the full hierarchy through transformed, preferably non-centered,
HMC/NUTS.
From a computational standpoint, the augmentation remains low-dimensional at the
group level, introducing one allowance variable and one amplitude variable per group in
addition to the standard regularized horseshoe components. Becauseτ,a g, andλ j enter
multiplicatively in the effective variance, inference is usually most stable when substantive
interpretation is concentrated onβ, the induced variancesv j,g, and the allowance parameters
κg, rather than on any single latent scale in isolation.
The key point is structural: the present hierarchy admits an exact conditional decom-
position, but several latent updates remain nonstandard. Accordingly, the computational
representation should be read as a faithful decomposition of the primitive model rather than
as a claim that the complete hierarchy is fully Gibbs-closed in elementary families.
1.11 Methodological summary
The grouped regularized horseshoe prior developed here is built around a simple organizing
principle: each layer of the hierarchy is assigned a primary role in the prior construction.
Parameter Primary role
τglobal sparsity calibration
λj coordinate-specific local escape
ag within-group amplitude modulation
κg group-specific allowance / retention gate
The global and local layers coincide with those of the standard regularized horseshoe.
The new ingredient is the allowance layerκ g, which induces a group-specific slab width
and serves as the main group-level control object in the present construction. These roles
are prior-structural rather than fully orthogonal posterior roles, since the effective variance
depends on the multiplicative combinationτ a gλj.
2 Marginal prior theory for the grouped regularized
horseshoe
This section studies the marginal prior behavior induced by the proposed grouped regularized
horseshoe hierarchy. The main purpose is to clarify, in a precise analytical sense, how the
new group-specific allowance layer modifies the usual regularized horseshoe backbone.
The results in this section are layered, and it is important to keep the three levels of
marginalization separate.

First, conditional on both the combined global and group-amplitude scales g :=τ a g and
the group-specific slab widthc g, the proposed prior retains the two signature features of the
regularized horseshoe: a logarithmic singularity at the origin and finite-slab regularization
in the tails.
Second, after the allowance layer is integrated out whiles g is held fixed, the fixed-slab
tail is replaced by a polynomial tail whose index is controlled by the upper-end Beta shape
parameterβ κ.
Third, a further fully marginal tail or origin statement after integrating overS=τ a g
requires additional moment conditions and is therefore auxiliary rather than automatic under
the default half-Cauchy / half-normal hierarchy. Accordingly, the fixed-(s, c) results and
the fixed-sallowance-marginal results are the primary structural statements of this section;
the fully marginal extensions later in the section should be read as conditional on extra
integrability.

2.6 Interpretation
The marginal prior theory developed above leads to a layered structural summary.
First, conditional ons=τ a g andc g, the grouped prior preserves the two defining reg-
ularized horseshoe features: a logarithmic spike at zero and finite-slab regularization in the
tails. This is the direct grouped analogue of the ordinary regularized horseshoe backbone.
Second, once the allowance layer is integrated out whilesis held fixed, the tail regime
changes. The fixed-slab exponential decay is replaced by a polynomial tail whose index
is controlled exactly by the Beta upper-end parameterβ κ. In this sense,κ g, through the
induced slab widthc g, determines how much tail protection is available to the coordinates
in groupg.
Third, the fully marginal origin and tail statements obtained after further integrating
overS=τ a g are auxiliary extensions under additional moment conditions. They clarify
how the conditional structure can propagate through further marginalization when suitable
integrability holds, but they are not automatic properties of the default half-Cauchy / half-
normal hierarchy.
Finally, whenp g = 1 anda g ≡1, the construction reduces exactly to the ordinary
regularized horseshoe.
Thus the proposed hierarchy may be viewed as a grouped regularized horseshoe with a
group-specific allowance mechanism: the regularized horseshoe backbone is retained, and
the new allowance layer randomizes the slab ceiling at the group level in a way that leaves
the conditional origin singularity order intact while converting the fixed-slab tail into a
Beta-controlled polynomial tail after marginalization over the allowance layer.
3 Posterior theory for the allowance parameter
This section develops posterior theory for the group-specific allowance parameterκ g. The
results are organized in two layers.
First, under the full grouped normal-means hierarchy, we establish exact structural re-
sults for the posterior ofκ g, including an exact bridge from posterior allowance to posterior
mean control, posterior propriety of the conditionalκ g-posterior, and a conditional posterior
geometry given the latent variance ratios.
Second, to obtain sharp asymptotic posterior concentration results, we study aprofile

role ofκ g as the group-level allowance parameter remains fully explicit. The resulting theory
yields null-group posterior contraction, signal-side localization and retention, simultaneous
groupwise separation under fixedG, and a direct lift to blockwise orthonormal grouped
regression.

Remark 3.36(Scope of the posterior theory).The exact bridge theorem, posterior propri-
ety, and the conditional posterior geometry in Subsection 3.1 hold under the full grouped
regularized horseshoe hierarchy in the grouped normal-means representation. By contrast,
the sharp posterior contraction, localization, retention, and simultaneous separation results
established in Subsections 3.3– 3.5 are profile-specialization results, and they rely on the one-
dimensional specializationλ j ≡1,a g ≡1, withτtreated as fixed. Under this specialization,
the posterior of eachκ g is exact and one-dimensional but generally nonconjugate, and the
posterior factorizes across groups, which is the mechanism used in the simultaneous separa-
tion and blockwise regression lift. These sharp results should therefore be read as posterior
theory for the allowance parameter in a canonical one-dimensional specialization of the full
grouped hierarchy, rather than as a full posterior contraction theory for the complete model
with random local, group-amplitude, and global-scale layers.

A.6 Implementation remarks
The results above show that the GR-RHS hierarchy preserves the key computational feature
of regularized horseshoe-type priors: conditional on the latent scales, the coefficient block
remains Gaussian. This makes block updates forβstraightforward and provides a natural
foundation for either Gaussian-block Gibbs updates or gradient-based samplers.
At the same time, several one-dimensional latent updates are nonstandard under the
present allowance layer. For this reason, the full conditional kernels in (120), (121), (122),
(123), and (124) are best viewed as explicit descriptions of the conditional structure rather
than as a prescription to use only Gibbs sampling. In practice, we recommend trans-
formed, preferably non-centered, HMC/NUTS as the primary implementation route. The
one-dimensional kernels derived above remain useful for exposing the computational anatomy
of the hierarchy and for designing auxiliary samplers where needed.
The key point is structural: the auxiliary-variable representations and transformed quan-
tities introduced in this appendix are exact computational devices. They do not alter the
primitive GR-RHS model stated in Section 1. At the same time, the appendix does not
provide a general theorem of full joint posterior propriety for the complete hierarchy under
arbitrary grouped designs. The results here should therefore be read as exact conditional-
structure statements rather than as a proof that the full model is globally Gibbs-closed.
